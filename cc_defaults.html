Our numeric columns have vastly different ranges (e.g. Age and credit limit). While this won't necessarily be a problem mathematically, it can make the results harder to interpret. As a result we'll normalise the numeric columnns around a mean of $0$:

In [6]:
cols_to_normalise = ["LIMIT_BAL", "AGE", "PAY_0", "PAY_2", "PAY_3", "PAY_4", "PAY_5", "PAY_6", 
                     "BILL_AMT1", "BILL_AMT2", "BILL_AMT3", "BILL_AMT4", "BILL_AMT5", "BILL_AMT6", 
                     "PAY_AMT1", "PAY_AMT2", "PAY_AMT3", "PAY_AMT4", "PAY_AMT5", "PAY_AMT6"]
data[cols_to_normalise] = data[cols_to_normalise].apply(lambda x: (x - np.mean(x))/np.std(x))

We're going to convert the categorical columns to dummy later, but for now let's replace the integer values with their actual meanings, to make the data easier to interpret:

The PAY_x columns should be categorical really. We'll ensure they're all positive-valued first:

In [9]:
pay_cols = ["PAY_0", "PAY_2", "PAY_3", "PAY_4", "PAY_5", "PAY_6"]
data[pay_cols] = data[pay_cols].apply(lambda x: x + 2)

And split our columns into independent and dependent variables:

In [10]:
X = data.iloc[:,0:23]
y = data.default

We need to convert our categorical columns to a series of binary columns or dummy columns. Thanks to StackExchange for this solution, passing the indices of the columns to convert to a pair of sklearn functions:

In [11]:
preprocessor = make_column_transformer((OneHotEncoder(),[1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10]),remainder="passthrough")
X = preprocessor.fit_transform(X)

And now we're ready to divide up the data into training and testing sets:

In [12]:
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.3)

After all that wrangling, training the model is very simple with sklearn:
