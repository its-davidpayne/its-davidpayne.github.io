In [2]:
user_data <- as.data.frame(read.csv("user_modelling.csv"))

The source data contains 5 independent variables (each a decimal value from 0 to 1) and one dependent variable (categorical: one of Very Low, Low, Medium, or High).

The first variable, STG, represents the amount of study time for the specific goal.

SCG is the degree of repetition of the goal object.

STR is the amount of study time for related materials.

LPR is the exam performance for related materials.

PEG is the exam performance for the specific goal.

UNS - the dependent variable - represents the knowledge value of the user.

For our purposes we will limit our analysis to 3 independent variables. This will allow us to visualise the output, at the cost of less accuracy. We will discard STR and LPR as they measure performance of related tasks, whereas the other variables measure performance of goal-specific tasks.

user_matrix <- as.matrix(cleaned_data)

Now, we plan to apply a K-Means clustering to the data so the next step is to select a value for K. To do this, we will calculate the Within Sum of Squares value for each possible K, from 1 to 12.

In [6]:
n <- 12
wss <- numeric(n)

for (k in 1:n) {
    wss[k] <- sum(kmeans(user_matrix, centers=k, nstart=25)$withinss)
}

Plotting the values of wss we just created, we can select an appropriate value for K.

In [7]:
plot(1:12, wss, type="b", xlab="Number of clusters", ylab="WSS")

It seems K = 4 to K = 7 would be reasonable. We will choose K = 4 to avoid overfitting (where the clustering is too fine), and because it simplifies our output.

With this specific dataset we also know that our dependent variable is a categorical factor with 4 levels so K = 4 is the obvious choice. In a real world setting of course that knowledge would not be available to us.
