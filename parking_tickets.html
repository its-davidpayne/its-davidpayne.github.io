We'll make a new data frame with the count already in a log10 scale, and with the field name capitalised. Though we won't use this df immediately, it will be useful later when we create a map.

In [8]:
parking_wards <- count(ward_list, ward)
parking_wards$n <- log10(parking_wards$n)
colnames(parking_wards)[colnames(parking_wards)=="ward"] <- "Ward"
# or we can let ggplot do the conversion in the next cell. I'll keep this log10 df for later mapping though.

As part of the call to ggplot in the cell below we will also (in order):

colour the bars, request a log 10 scale and specific y-axis labels, rotate the x-axis labels, specify axis titles, and limit the scope of the y-axis.

In [9]:
library(scales)
ggplot(ward_list, mapping=aes(x=ward_list$ward)) +
    geom_bar(fill="blue") +
    scale_y_log10(breaks=c(100, 1000, 10000, 100000), labels=comma) +
    theme(axis.text.x=element_text(angle=90, hjust=1)) +
    labs(x="Ward", y="Count (log10 scale)") +
    coord_cartesian(ylim=c(50, 100000))

Now we have something usable. We can see that the vast majority of tickets are issued in the city centre. The next most frequent areas are also inner-city urban wards, while the suburban areas further from the centre tend to attract fewer tickets. This accords with our intuition, as both parking restrictions AND Parking Enforcement Officer patrols are likely to be more common the closer to the city centre you go. However, the sheer scale of the disparity (less obvious with a logarithmic y-axis) is surprising and might lead one to conclude that extra attention should be paid by enforcement officers to the wards further from the city centre.

Note that we trimmed the y-axis to only show 10,000 to 14,000. This allows us to see more detail in the differences between months, at the expense of the data being slightly less easy to understand at first glance.

The data shows a general trend of fewest tickets in the summer holiday period and most during the winter holiday period. This accords with our expectation as summer will tend to see more people taking vacations from work, and winter is likely to see more people shopping (especially in the city centre), leading to increased competition for legitimate parking spaces.

That looks very promising. The only major change we need is to label the wards. For that we'll use the other shapefile we imported with points for all ward centroids. While it's possible to do this programatically in R, for a small dataset such as this with around 30 points it's probably quicker to do manually, not to mention more accurate.

In [22]:
library(RColorBrewer)
ggplot(data=map_plot, mapping=aes(x=long, y=lat)) +
  geom_polygon(aes(group=group, fill=n), color="black", size=0.5) +
  scale_fill_distiller(name="Tickets (log10 scale)", palette="RdYlGn") +
  theme_void() + 
  geom_text(data=namesdf, aes(x=coords.x1, y=coords.x2, label=Ward), size=2) +
  theme(legend.position=c(0.8, 0.2), plot.title=element_text(size=18, hjust=0.5)) +
  ggtitle("Parking Ticket frequency by Ward")

This visualisation confirms my earlier statement: that number of tickets is inversely correlated to distance from the city centre. Though I was able to tell this from the bar chart, someone unfamiliar with Manchester's wards would need this map to observe the same relationship.
